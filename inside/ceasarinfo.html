Caesar Cipher

The Caesar cipher, also known as the shift cipher, is one of the simplest and most widely known encryption techniques. It is a type of substitution cipher in which each letter in the plaintext is shifted a certain number of places down or up the alphabet. The key in a Caesar cipher is the number of positions each letter is shifted.

Example

Suppose we want to encrypt the message "HELLO" with a Caesar cipher and a key of 3. Here's how it works:

  • H is shifted 3 positions to the right and becomes E.
  • E is shifted 3 positions to the right and becomes B.
  • L is shifted 3 positions to the right and becomes I.
  • L is shifted 3 positions to the right and becomes I.
  • O is shifted 3 positions to the right and becomes L.
So, "HELLO" is encrypted as "EBIIL" with a Caesar cipher and a key of 3.

Decryption

Decryption in the Caesar cipher is done by shifting the letters in the opposite direction. If we have the encrypted message "EBIIL" and we know the key is 3, we simply shift each letter 3 positions to the left to recover the original message "HELLO".

Key Variations

The key in a Caesar cipher can be any integer, positive or negative. Positive keys shift the letters to the right, while negative keys shift the letters to the left. The key can also be greater than 26 (the number of letters in the English alphabet). In such cases, the alphabet wraps around, and the key is taken modulo 26. For example, a key of 29 is equivalent to a key of 3 (29 % 26 = 3).
